Mismatch repair testing


All rectal cancers should be tested for
mutations (changes) in genes that normally
find and fix damaged DNA, called mismatch
repair (MMR) genes. Testing involves
analyzing a piece of the rectal tumor in a lab.


Depending on the method used, an abnormal
result is called either mismatch repair
deficiency (dMMR) or microsatellite instabilityhigh (MSI-H). These cancers have a high
number of mutated (changed) genes and
respond differently to rectal cancer treatments.


Most colorectal cancers don't have either of
these features and are described as mismatch
repair proficient (pMMR) or microsatellite
stable (MSS).

Family health history


Your provider will ask about the health history
of your biological (related by blood) family
members. People with a first-degree relative
with colorectal cancer are more likely to have
this cancer than those without a family history
of colorectal cancer.


Most colorectal cancers occur for unknown
reasons. A small number of people are born
with a disorder that makes them more likely
to get colorectal and other cancers. These
are called inherited cancer syndromes. Two
such conditions include Lynch syndrome and
familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP).


People born with Lynch syndrome are at high
risk of developing colorectal, endometrial,
ovarian, and several less common cancers.
Lynch syndrome is caused by inherited
mutations of the MMR genes. For cancers
found to have the dMMR or MSI-H biomarker,
testing for Lynch syndrome is recommended.


FAP causes many polyps to form in the
colon and rectum. The polyps start as benign
growths but can become invasive colorectal
cancer over time. Cancer often develops by
age 50 in people with FAP.


Genetic testing for hereditary
cancer risk

Gene differences you are born with are called
germline variants. If an inherited cancer
or cancer syndrome is suspected, your
provider may refer you to a genetic counselor.
This expert can speak with you and your
family about testing for syndromes related to
colorectal cancer.

PI3K pathway testing


Some rectal tumors have alterations in genes
in the PI3K pathway. Genes in this pathway
include _PI3KCA_ and _PTEN_ . Changes in these
genes can activate an enzyme (PI3K) that
causes the cancer to grow quickly and return
after treatment.


Cancer experts have found that aspirin helps
prevent recurrence (after recommended
treatment) in cancers with these changes.


Confirm with your care team that the rectal
tumor will be tested for somatic (nonhereditary) alterations that activate PI3K.


_DPYD_ testing


Capecitabine and fluorouracil (5-FU)
are chemotherapy medicines called
fluoropyrimidines. They are often used to treat
colorectal cancer. Your body uses a special
enzyme (protein) called DPD to break down
these medicines so they can work properly and
leave your body safely and efficiently.


Around 1 in 15 people inherit a difference in
the gene that makes DPD, the _DPYD_ gene.
This leads to less DPD protein being made,
called DPD deficiency. In these people,
fluoropyrimidines can build up to the point of
causing severe and sometimes life-threatening
side effects, referred to as fluoropyrimidine
toxicity.


Signs and symptoms often start within several
days of the first fluoropyrimidine treatment.
Symptoms include mouth sores, abdominal
pain, blood in the stool, nausea, vomiting,
and diarrhea. The skin on the palms of your

Blood tests


CEA blood test

Carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) is a protein
found in blood. People with colorectal cancer
tend to have more of this protein than normal.


Monitoring CEA can be helpful for some
cancers that are only in the rectum. If the
level rises, it could signal that the cancer has
spread. If the level is high at diagnosis, it could
suggest that the cancer has already spread.
Monitoring CEA isn't helpful for everyone.
Pregnant people and tobacco users may also
have more CEA than average in their blood.


Complete blood count

Cancer and other health problems can cause
low or high blood cell counts. A complete blood
count (CBC) measures the number of white
blood cells, red blood cells, and platelets in a
blood sample.


White blood cells help you fight infection. Red
blood cells carry oxygen throughout the body.
Platelets help wounds heal by forming blood
clots.


Chemistry profile

A chemistry profile (also called a
comprehensive metabolic panel) is a group
of tests that provides information about how
well your kidneys, liver, and other organs are
working.

3 Treatment for early rectal cancer


Endoscopic submucosal
dissection


Endoscopic submucosal dissection (ESD) is
a minimally invasive procedure that removes
small rectal cancers in one piece, often curing
them.


ESD is a recommended option for low-risk
T1 tumors as long as nearby lymph nodes
appear cancer-free on imaging tests. T1 is the
staging term for tumors that haven’t grown into
the muscle layer of the rectal wall. Low-risk
describes tumors without specific features that
often lead to recurrence.


First, an endoscope is used to locate the tumor
in the rectal wall. A tool is guided through the
scope to inject fluid between the tumor and the
layer of muscle beneath it. This helps lift and
separate the tumor, making it easier to cut off.

Transanal local excision


If the tumor is located at the end of the rectum,

aren’t removed.

3 Treatment for early rectal cancer


Abdominal surgery


This type of surgery, also called
transabdominal resection, involves cutting
into the abdomen to reach and remove the
rectal tumor, some surrounding healthy tissue,
and nearby lymph nodes. It is the preferred
treatment for the following rectal tumors:


 - T1 tumors with high-risk features

 - All T2 tumors


T2 is the staging term for tumors that have
invaded the muscle layer of the rectal wall.


Abdominal surgery is also a recommended
option for low-risk T1 tumors and some lowrisk, larger (T3) tumors that haven't spread
to lymph nodes.


The following types of abdominal surgery are
described on the next pages:


 - Low anterior resection (LAR)

 - Abdominoperineal resection (APR)


The type of abdominal surgery performed
depends on the location of the tumor and
the extent of the cancer. At least 12 lymph
nodes should be removed during all types
of transabdominal surgery.


The tissue and lymph nodes removed
during surgery are sent to a lab for testing. A
pathologist assesses how far within the rectal
wall the cancer has grown and whether any
removed lymph nodes have cancer.


Using this information, they assign the cancer
a stage of I (1), II (2), or III (3). This process
is called surgical (or pathologic) staging. The

treatment is needed.


Stage 1 cancer has grown into the second

Stage 2 cancer has grown through the

5 Treatment for metastatic rectal cancer


Planning treatment for metastatic rectal

cancer starts with biomarker testing.


If not found and treated early enough, cancer
cells will break off from the rectal tumor and
travel in lymph or blood to other areas of the
body—usually the liver, lungs, or abdomen.
This process is called metastasis. Treatment
for metastatic rectal cancer depends on the
results of biomarker testing, described next.


Biomarker testing


Biomarkers are features of a cancer that can
help guide your care. Many biomarkers are
mutations (changes) in specific genes.


When possible, biomarker testing is performed
on a piece of tumor tissue removed during a
biopsy or surgery. Or, if needed, a sample of
your blood can be tested instead.


All metastatic rectal cancers should be tested
for the following biomarkers:


 - _KRAS_ and _NRAS_ mutations

 - _BRAF_ V600E mutations

 - HER2 amplification

 - Mismatch repair (MMR) or microsatellite
instability (MSI) status, if unknown

Testing for many biomarkers at one time is
called multigene panel testing (MGPT). MGPT
can find other rare biomarkers for which
targeted therapies are available, such as
_POLE/POLD1_ mutations, _RET_ gene fusions,
and _NTRK_ gene fusions.

Local therapies


Your options for treating liver and lung tumors
may include local therapies. These are
treatments that remove or destroy tumors
without damaging the surrounding area.


Some are interventional oncology/radiology
techniques, also known as image-guided
therapies. These techniques use imaging,
such as ultrasound, CT, or PET, to deliver
minimally invasive cancer treatments. Using
imaging during the procedure allows your
doctor to precisely target the tumor(s) and
confirm treatment success.


To learn if treatment with surgery or other
local therapies is an option, your case should
be reviewed by a multidisciplinary team of
experts. This team should include a surgeon
experienced in removing liver and lung tumors
and an interventional oncologist/radiologist.


Resection

Surgery to remove liver or lung tumors is
called resection. Resection may be paired with
ablation (described next) to completely remove
small tumors.


If a liver resection is needed, your liver may
need to be enlarged first. This is done using a
minimally invasive procedure called portal vein
embolization.


Tumor ablation

Image-guided ablation is a type of treatment
that destroys small liver or lung tumors with
minimal damage to surrounding normal tissue.
It may be paired with surgery to completely
remove a tumor or used alone for small tumors
that can be fully removed or destroyed.

Supportive care is an important part of cancer

Supportive care includes a wide range of

 - Making treatment decisions

 - Coordinating your care

Clinical trials


A clinical trial is a type of medical research
study. After being developed and tested in a
lab, potential new ways of fighting cancer need
to be studied in people.


If found to be safe and effective in a clinical
trial, a drug, device, or treatment approach
may be approved by the U.S. FDA.


Everyone with cancer should carefully consider
all of the treatment options available for their
cancer type, including standard treatments and
clinical trials. Talk to your doctor about whether
a clinical trial may make sense for you.


Phases

Most cancer clinical trials focus on treatment
and are done in phases.


 - Phase 1 trials study the safety and
side effects of an investigational drug or
treatment approach.

 - Phase 2 trials study how well the drug or
approach works against a specific type of
cancer.

 - Phase 3 trials test the drug or approach
against a standard treatment. If the results
are good, it may be approved by the FDA.

 - Phase 4 trials study the safety and
benefit of an FDA-approved treatment.


5 Treatment for metastatic rectal cancer


Who can enroll?

It depends on the clinical trial’s rules, called
eligibility criteria. The rules may be about age,
cancer type and stage, treatment history, or
general health. They ensure that participants
are alike in specific ways and that the trial is as
safe as possible for the participants.


Informed consent

Clinical trials are managed by a research
team. This group of experts will review the
study with you in detail, including its purpose
and the risks and benefits of joining. All of this
information is also provided in an informed
consent form. Read the form carefully and
ask questions before signing it. Take time to
discuss it with people you trust. Keep in mind
that you can leave and seek treatment outside
of the clinical trial at any time.


Will I get a placebo?

Placebos (inactive versions of real medicines)
are almost never used alone in cancer clinical
trials. It is common to receive either a placebo
with a standard treatment, or a new drug with
a standard treatment. You will be informed,
verbally and in writing, if a placebo is part of a
clinical trial before you enroll.


Are clinical trials free?

There is no fee to enroll in a clinical trial. The
study sponsor pays for research-related costs,
including the study drug. But you may need
to pay for other services, like transportation or
childcare, due to extra appointments. During
the trial, you will continue to receive standard
cancer care. This care is often covered by
insurance.

6 Survivorship


Survivorship begins on the day you learn

you have rectal cancer. It focuses on the

physical, emotional, and financial issues

unique to cancer survivors.


Rectal cancer survivors may experience both
short- and long-term health effects of cancer
and its treatment. The effects depend in part
on the treatment(s) received. Surgery, anticancer medicines, and radiation therapy all
have unique potential side effects.


Bowel changes


Surgery that involves removing all or part of
the rectum is called a low anterior resection
(LAR). After this surgery, it’s common to
experience changes in your bowel movements
and habits. These symptoms, listed below,
are referred to as low anterior resection
syndrome (LARS):


 - Increase in bowel movements

 - Urgent need to pass stool

 - Having many (possibly watery) bowel
movements over the course of a few
hours (stool clustering)

 - Irregular and unpredictable bowel
movements

 - Inability to control bowel movements
(fecal incontinence)

 - Increase in gas (flatulence)


NCCN Guidelines for Patients [®]
Rectal Cancer, 2026 47



Loperamide (Imodium) is an over-the-counter
anti-diarrheal medicine. It can help with stool
clustering and incontinence. Exercises that
strengthen the muscles in the pelvic floor can
help with urgency and stool incontinence.


Making changes to your diet can also help with
LARS symptoms. Eat foods high in insoluble
(not soluble) fiber and use fiber supplements.
Psyllium-based products (like Metamucil) may
help slow and thicken the stool.


Ask your care team for a list of foods that
may help or worsen bowel changes after
low anterior resection. Many people find that
protective undergarments provide peace of
mind. A cancer nutritionist can also help you
make changes to your diet after rectal surgery.


Nerve damage


The chemotherapy drug oxaliplatin can
damage nerves in your fingers and toes.
Symptoms include numbness, cramping,
tingling, or pain in these areas. Acupuncture
and heat may help.


If you have painful nerve damage, a drug
called duloxetine (Cymbalta) may help. If the
pain is persistent, talk to your doctor about
seeing a pain management specialist.


6 Survivorship


Ostomy care


If you have an ostomy, you may want to join
an ostomy support group. Another option is to
see a health care provider that specializes in
ostomy care, such as an ostomy nurse.


People with ostomies can still live very active
lifestyles. However, it’s a good idea to talk
to an ostomy professional before doing any
intense physical activity.


Sexual health


Many people experience sexual side effects
after chemotherapy and radiation. For
those with a penis, this could mean difficulty
getting an erection. Those with a vagina may
experience vaginal dryness and pain during
sex.

**or seeing an ostomy**

Radiation therapy to the pelvis can cause the
vagina to become shorter and narrower. This
is called vaginal stenosis. Vaginal stenosis
can make it uncomfortable or even painful to
have sex, or to have vaginal examinations by
a doctor. Vaginal dilator therapy can help. A
vaginal dilator is a device used to gradually
stretch or widen the vagina.


The size of the dilator can be increased over
time as the vagina lengthens and widens. Use
of a vaginal dilator can begin as soon as you
are healed after treatment.


Using a dilator during radiation treatment
sessions can also help maintain sexual health
after treatment.

Early menopause


Menopausal hormone therapy

The ovaries make hormones needed for
menstruation and natural pregnancy. Radiation
damages the ovaries, causing hormone levels
to drop. This is known as premature ovarian
insufficiency (POI), or radiation-induced
premature menopause. It causes infertility and
may also cause symptoms of menopause,
including:


 - Stopping of periods

 - Hot flashes

 - Sleeping problems

 - Night sweats

 - Weight gain

 - Changes in mood

 - Vaginal dryness


Menopausal hormone therapy may help lessen
these side effects. This approach used to be
called hormone replacement therapy, or HRT.


One option is systemic combination therapy.
Both estrogen and progestogen are given,
usually either as a pill or a patch placed on the
skin. The hormones travel throughout the body
in the bloodstream. Progestogen is included
because giving estrogen by itself can increase
the risk of uterine cancer in those with a
uterus.


Another option for menopausal hormone
therapy is vaginal estrogen cream or tablets.
This type may be the best option for symptoms
that mainly affect the vagina, such as dryness.

symptom team may be helpful to determine

Paying for care


Cancer survivors face a unique financial
burden. The cost of cancer care can become
unmanageable, especially for those with little
or no health insurance. You may also have
costs not directly related to treatment, such as
travel expenses and the cost of childcare.


The term financial toxicity is used to describe
the problems patients face related to the cost
of medical care. Financial toxicity can affect
your quality of life and access to needed health
care.


If you need help paying for cancer care,
financial assistance may be available. Talk with
a patient navigator, your care team’s social
worker, and your hospital’s financial services
department.


6 Survivorship


**Healthy habits for cancer survivors**


A low sugar (called glycemic load, or GL) diet may help
prevent the return of rectal cancer. Low GL foods cause
a slower and smaller rise in blood sugar levels than other
carbohydrate-containing foods.


Talk to your doctor about the possible risks and benefits
of long-term aspirin therapy to help prevent the return of
colorectal cancers.


Follow your primary care provider’s recommendations for
screening for other cancers based on your age, sex, and risk
level.


Get other recommended health care for your age, such
as blood pressure checks, hepatitis C screening, and
immunizations (like the flu shot).


Eat a diet rich in plant-based foods. Limit red meat and
processed foods. Ask your provider for a referral to a cancer
nutritionist.


Alcohol can increase the risk of some cancers. Drink little to
no alcohol.


Move more, rest less. Try to exercise at a moderate intensity
for at least 150 minutes per week.


Maintain a healthy weight. Tracking your weight, food intake,
and activity levels may help you meet your goals.

Words to know


**abdominoperineal resection (APR)**
A type of transabdominal surgery used to
remove tumors in the lower rectum. Involves
removing the rectum and anus and creating a
permanent colostomy for stool to leave the body.

**ablation**
A local therapy used to destroy tumors in the
liver or lungs. Also called image-guided thermal
ablation.

**adenocarcinoma**
Cancer that starts in the glands that line your
organs. The most common type of rectal cancer.

**adenoma**
The most common type of colorectal polyp and
the most likely to form cancer cells. Also called
adenomatous polyp.

**biomarkers**
Specific features of cancer cells used to guide
treatment. Biomarkers are often mutations
(changes) in the DNA of the cancer cells.

**CAPEOX**
A chemotherapy regimen that includes
capecitabine and oxaliplatin.

**carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA)**
A protein released by some tumors that can be
detected in blood.

**chemoembolization**
Treatment for metastatic liver tumors that
delivers spheres carrying chemotherapy to the
liver vessels supplying the tumor.

**colon**
The first and longest section of the large bowel.
Unused food is turned into stool in the colon.


NCCN Guidelines for Patients [®]
Rectal Cancer, 2026 56



**colostomy**
Surgery to connect a part of the colon to the
outside of the abdomen and that allows stool to
drain into a bag.

**endoscopic submucosal dissection (ESD)**
A minimally invasive type of surgery used to
remove small rectal tumors in one piece. Also
called endoscopic mucosal resection (EMR).

**external beam radiation therapy (EBRT)**
Treatment with high-energy rays received from a
machine outside the body.

**familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP)**
An inherited cancer syndrome that increases the
risk of colorectal cancer.

**FOLFIRI**
A chemotherapy regimen used for some
advanced colorectal cancers. Includes
leucovorin calcium, fluorouracil, and irinotecan.

**FOLFIRINOX**
A chemotherapy regimen used for some
colorectal cancers. Includes leucovorin calcium
(folinic acid), fluorouracil, irinotecan, and
oxaliplatin.

**FOLFOX**
A chemotherapy regimen that includes
leucovorin calcium, fluorouracil, and oxaliplatin.

**interventional oncology/radiology**
A medical specialty that uses imaging techniques
to deliver minimally invasive cancer treatments.

**large intestine (bowel)**
A long, tube-shaped organ that forms the last
part of the digestive system. Includes the colon,
rectum, and anus.


Words to know


**lymph**
A clear fluid containing white blood cells.

**low anterior resection (LAR)**
A type of transabdominal surgery for removing
tumors in the middle or upper rectum. Involves
removing the rectal tumor and part or all of the
last section of the colon. The colon is connected
to the remaining rectum (when possible) or
attached directly to the anus.

**lymph node**
Small groups of disease-fighting cells located
throughout the body.

**metastasis**
The spread of cancer cells from the first
(primary) tumor to a distant site.

**mismatch repair deficiency (dMMR)/high**
**microsatellite instability (MSI-H)**
A biomarker (feature) of some colorectal cancers
that is used to guide treatment. All colorectal
cancers should be tested for this biomarker.

**mismatch repair proficient (pMMR)/**
**microsatellite stable (MSS)**
Describes cancers that don't have the mismatch
repair deficiency (dMMR) biomarker or the
microsatellite instability-high (MSI-H) biomarker.

**pathologist**
A doctor who specializes in testing cells and
tissue to find disease.

**polyp**
An overgrowth of cells on the inner lining of the
colon wall. Pedunculated polyps stick out from
the rectal wall, while sessile polyps are flatter.

**portal vein embolization**
A minimally invasive procedure to enlarge
the liver. May be needed if a liver resection is
planned.

**radioembolization**
A treatment for liver tumors that uses radioactive
spheres injected in the hepatic (liver) artery
(the blood vessel supplying tumor). Also called
selective internal radiation therapy, or SIRT.

**rectum**
An organ in the digestive system that holds stool
until it exits the body through the anus.

**recurrence**
The return of cancer after a cancer-free period.

**resectable**
Describes a cancer that can be safely removed
using surgery.

**stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT)**
A highly specialized type of radiation therapy.

spread to the liver, lungs, or bone.

**supportive care**
A range of resources and care to improve quality

**systemic therapy**
The use of medicines that enter the bloodstream.
Chemotherapy, targeted therapy, and
immunotherapy are types of systemic therapy.

**unresectable**
Describes a cancer that can't be safely removed
using surgery.
