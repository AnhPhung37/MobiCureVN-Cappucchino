Introduction

## Colorectal cancer is a major cause of morbidity and mortality in Australia.

### It is the second most common cancer diagnosed in both men and women, and is more common in those aged over 50 years. Colorectal cancer is also the second most common cause of cancer death and accounts for 9% of all cancer deaths. [[1]]


This profile of colorectal cancer in Australia highlights the need for guidelines to ensure
clinical best practice in its prevention, detection and management.

Recommendations – PRIMARY PREVENTION

## Primary prevention

### Dietary and lifestyle strategies




### The use of aspirin for prevention of colorectal cancer






|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|For all people aged 50–70 years who are at average risk of colorectal cancer, aspirin should be actively<br>considered to prevent colorectal cancer. A low dose (100–300 mg per day) is recommended for at least 2.5<br>years, commencing at age 50 to 70 years. The beneft may extend to older ages with longer duration of use.<br>Beneft for cancer prevention (though shorter for cardiovascular risk) is evident only 10 years after initiation so a<br>life expectancy of at least 10 years should be taken into consideration in the advice to use aspirin.<br>The choice to take aspirin should be personalised based on age, sex and potential reduction in cardiovascular<br>events, cerebrovascular events and thrombotic stroke. The individual should take into account the potential<br>risks of taking aspirin. Aspirin should be avoided in patients with current dyspepsia, any history of peptic ulcer,<br>aspirin allergy, bleeding diathesis, an increased risk of gastrointestinal haemorrhage (such as associated with<br>use of oral anticoagulants or antiplatelet agents), or renal impairment.<br>The beneft in colorectal cancer risk reduction in women over 65 is less clear cut. However, based on limited<br>data available, older women with cardiovascular risk factors may derive a greater overall beneft than harm.|**B**|







|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|People who are at high risk of colorectal cancer due to Lynch Syndrome carrier status should be advised to<br>begin aspirin from the commencement of their colonoscopy screening (usually at age 25 years).|**A**|

## Population screening for colorectal cancer






|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|**Overall population screening strategy**<br>The recommended strategy for population screening in Australia, directed at those at average risk of colorectal<br>cancer and without relevant symptoms, is immunochemical faecal occult blood testing every 2 years, starting at<br>age 50 years and continuing to age 74 years.|**C**|








|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|**Primary screening test**<br>An immunochemical faecal occult blood test is recommended as the screening modality for the detection of<br>colorectal cancer in the average-risk population.|**C**|








|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|**Primary screening test**<br>The emerging faecal, blood or serum tests for cancer-specifc biomarkers such as DNA are not recommended<br>as population screening modalities for colorectal cancer.|**C**|








|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|**Primary screening test**<br>The use of fexible sigmoidoscopy as a primary screening test is not recommended for population screening in<br>the average-risk population.|**C**|








|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|**frequency of testing**<br>Population screening for colorectal cancer using immunochemical faecal blood testing every 2 years is<br>recommended. It is not recommended that the frequency of screening within the NBCSP be increased to yearly.|**N/A**|








|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|**target age group**<br>It is recommended that the age range for organised population screening continues to be 50–74 years.|**N/A**|





|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|**target age group**<br>Starting at age 40 is not recommended for population screening as it is unlikely to be cost-effective.|**N/A**|

### Optimal maximum time from referral to diagnosis and treatment (diagnostic interval)






|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|For patients with symptoms suggestive of colorectal cancer, the total time from frst healthcare presentation† <br>to diagnostic colonoscopy should be no more than 120 days. Diagnostic intervals greater than 120 days are<br>associated with poorer clinical outcomes.<br>†    First healthcare presentation is defned as the date of presentation in general practice with symptoms suggestive of colorectal cancer or positive<br>iFOBT for screening.|**C**|








|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|A diagnostic interval of 120 days should be the maximum time from frst healthcare presentation† to diagnostic<br>colonoscopy for triage Categories 1 and 2, whether it is for a patient with symptoms or after a positive iFOBT<br>used for colorectal cancer screening. Diagnostic intervals greater than 120 days are associated with poorer<br>clinical outcomes.<br>†    First healthcare presentation is defned as the date of presentation in general practice with symptoms suggestive of colorectal cancer or positive<br>iFOBT for screening.|**D**|

Recommendations – RISK AND SCREEning based on family history

## Risk and screening based on family history

### Strength of association between family history and colorectal cancer risk






|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|**Category 1**<br>People who have one relative with colorectal cancer diagnosed at age 55 or older should be advised that their<br>own risk of developing colorectal cancer could be up to twice the average risk, but is still not high enough to<br>justify CRC screening by colonoscopy.|**C**|








|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|**Category 2**<br>People should be advised that their risk of developing colorectal cancer is at least three times higher than<br>average, but could be up to six times higher than average, if they have any of the following:<br>• 	 one frst-degree relative with colorectal cancer diagnosed before age 55 years<br>• 	 two frst-degree relatives with colorectal cancer diagnosed at any age<br>•   one frst-degree relative and at least two second-degree relative diagnosed with colorectal cancer at any age.|**C**|








|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|**Category 3**<br>People should be advised that their risk of colorectal cancer is at least seven times higher than average, but<br>could be up to 10 times higher than average, if they have either of the following:<br>• 	 at least three frst-degree or second-degree relatives with colorectal cancer, with at least one diagnosed<br>before age 55 years<br>• 	 at least three frst-degree relatives with colorectal cancer diagnosed at any age.|**C**|

## Elective and emergency surgery for colon and rectal cancer

### Optimal approach to resection of colon cancers


|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|Either an open approach or a laparoscopic approach can be used for the resection of colon cancer.|**D**|






|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|Laparoscopic colectomy has post-operative advantages over open colectomy and should be performed when<br>the surgical expertise and hospital infrastructure are available.|**D**|










### Optimal approach to resection of rectal cancers






|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|Open surgery is the standard approach for resection of rectal cancer. Laparoscopic resection can be<br>considered in selected cases if the surgical expertise (including advanced laparoscopic skills) and hospital<br>infrastructure are available noting that it is a technique that has yet to be proven safe and effcacious in all<br>patients for rectal cancer.|**C**|

### Effective treatment for early rectal cancer






|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|For patients with stage 1 rectal cancer (T1/2, N0, M0), cases should be discussed by a multidisciplinary team<br>to determine optimal management with respect to risk of local recurrence, avoidance of a permanent stoma,<br>and ftness for surgery.|**C**|








|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|For patients with T1 tumours local excision can be considered, provided that the tumour can be removed with<br>clear margins and that the treating clinician counsels the patient that:<br>•  	the risk of local recurrence increases as the T1 tumour stage progresses (from T1sm1 to T1sm2, or from<br>T1sm2 to T1sm3)<br>•  	radical resection may be required after histopathological review of the local excision specimen.|**D**|




|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|For patients with T2 tumours, consider radical resection as the frst option if they are ft for surgery.|**C**|

### Stenting or colostomy vs. acute resection with primary anastomosis in acute obstruction due to left-sided colon or rectal carcinoma






|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|In patients with acute obstruction due to left-sided colorectal cancer who are potentially curative, the use of<br>stenting as a bridge to surgery is not recommended as standard treatment, due to the potential risk of tumour<br>perforation and conversion of a curative case to a palliative case.|**D**|

### Patients with locally recurrent colon or rectal cancer suitable for curative surgery






|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|For patients with isolated local recurrence of rectal cancer, consider referral to a centre with the necessary<br>expertise to perform curative surgery (also known as pelvic exenteration).|**D**|








|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|Re-operative surgery for locally recurrent rectal cancer should only be offered after due consideration of, and<br>discussion with the patient about, the potential survival advantage, quality-of-life outcomes, and potential<br>treatment-related morbidity.|**D**|






















### Patients with resectable synchronous or metachronous metastatic colon or rectal cancer suitable for curative surgery






|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|In patients with resectable liver metastases, liver resection should be offered, as this improves overall and<br>progression free survival.|**D**|





|Evidence-based recommendation|Grade|
|---|---|
|Patients referred for liver resection should be counselled about the potential complications associated with liver<br>resection in comparison with non-curative treatments.|**D**|
