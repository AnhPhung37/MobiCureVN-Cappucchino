2 Testing for colon cancer


Mismatch repair testing


All colon cancers should be tested for
alterations (changes) in genes that normally
find and fix damaged DNA, called mismatch
repair (MMR) genes. Testing involves
analyzing a piece of the colon tumor in a lab.


Depending on the testing method used, an
abnormal result is called either mismatch
repair deficiency (dMMR) or microsatellite
instability (MSI-H). These cancers have a
high number of mutated (changed) genes and
respond differently to colon cancer treatments.


Most colon cancers don't have either of these
features and are described as mismatch repair
proficient (pMMR) or microsatellite stable
(MSS).


Family health history


Your provider will ask about the health history
of your biological (related by blood) family
members. People who have a first-degree
relative with colon cancer are more likely to
have this cancer than those without a family
history of colon cancer.


Most colon cancers occur for unknown
reasons. While rare, some people are born
with a disorder that makes them more likely
to get colon and other cancers. These are
called inherited cancer syndromes. Two such
conditions include Lynch syndrome and familial
adenomatous polyposis (FAP).


People born with Lynch syndrome are at high
risk of developing colorectal, endometrial, and
ovarian cancers. Lynch syndrome is caused by
inherited differences in the MMR genes. For
cancers found to have the dMMR or MSI-H

recommended.


FAP causes many polyps to form in the

growths, but can become invasive colon

age 50 in people with FAP.


Genetic testing for hereditary
cancer risk

If an inherited cancer or cancer syndrome is
suspected, your provider may refer you to a
genetic counselor. This expert can speak with
you and your family about being tested for
syndromes related to colon cancer.


To be tested, you must provide a sample of
blood or saliva. A pathologist tests the sample
for germline (hereditary) gene differences
that cause these syndromes. Speaking with
a genetic counselor is recommended for any
positive genetic testing result.


PI3K pathway testing


Some colon tumors have alterations in genes
in the PI3K pathway. Genes in this pathway
include _PI3KCA_ and _PTEN_ . Changes in these
genes can activate an enzyme (PI3K) that
causes the cancer to grow quickly and return
after treatment.


Cancer experts have found that in cancers
with these changes, taking aspirin daily for 3
years after surgery and other recommended
treatment helps prevent the return of cancer.


Confirm with your care team that the colon
tumor will be tested for somatic (nonhereditary) alterations that activate PI3K.

Capecitabine and fluorouracil (5-FU)

Blood tests


Bloodwork for suspected colon cancer typically

Invasive cancer


If the cancer has grown too deep into the colon
wall to be removed by polypectomy, colectomy
(surgery) is needed. Surgery is an option only
if the tumor can be completely removed.


If you can’t have surgery first because
of where the tumor is located or other
reasons, systemic therapy (chemotherapy
or immunotherapy) is recommended first.
Systemic therapy may also be recommended
first for advanced cancer, or cancer that has
spread to lymph nodes.


If systemic therapy is planned first,
chemotherapy is recommended for mismatch
repair proficient (pMMR) or microsatellite
stable (MSS) cancers. Immunotherapy with a
checkpoint inhibitor is preferred for cancers
with mismatch repair deficiency (dMMR) or
microsatellite instability (MSI-H).


Sometimes radiation therapy (alone or with
chemotherapy) is given along with systemic
therapy for unresectable cancers.

also remove the fatty tissue that supports the

will need to be removed together (en bloc)

3 Treatment for non-metastatic colon cancer


Staging


The stage describes the extent of cancer
in the body. The American Joint Committee
on Cancer (AJCC) tumor, node, metastasis
(TNM) system is used to stage colon cancer.
In the AJCC system, the following key pieces
of information about the cancer are used for
staging:


 - T: How far the tumor has grown into or
through the colon wall

 - N: Whether any lymph nodes have cancer

 - M: Whether the cancer has spread
to areas or organs outside the colon
(metastasized)


The T, N, and M scores are combined to
assign the cancer one of the following stages:
0, I (1), II (2), III (3), or IV (4).


Stage 0



**Team approach**


When possible, treatment for colon
cancer is best delivered by a team of
experts in the following specialties:


- Digestive health (gastroenterology)

- Chemotherapy (medical oncology)

- Surgery (surgical oncology)

- Radiotherapy (radiation oncology)

- Imaging (radiology)

If not found and treated early, cancer cells

Colon cancers are not all the same. Testing

treatments that are less likely to help.

Clinical trials


A clinical trial is a type of medical research
study. After being developed and tested in a
lab, potential new ways of fighting cancer need
to be studied in people.


If found to be safe and effective in a clinical
trial, a drug, device, or treatment approach
may be approved by the U.S. FDA.


Everyone with cancer should carefully consider
all of the treatment options available for their
cancer type, including standard treatments and
clinical trials. Talk to your doctor about whether

Phases

Most cancer clinical trials focus on treatment
and are done in phases.


 - Phase 1 trials study the safety and
side effects of an investigational drug or
treatment approach.

 - Phase 2 trials study how well the drug or
approach works against a specific type of
cancer.

 - Phase 3 trials test the drug or approach
against a standard treatment. If the results
are good, it may be approved by the FDA.

 - Phase 4 trials study the safety and
benefit of an FDA-approved treatment.


Who can enroll?

It depends on the clinical trial’s rules, called
eligibility criteria. The rules may be about age,
cancer type and stage, treatment history, or
general health. They ensure that participants
are alike in specific ways and that the trial is as
safe as possible for the participants.

Nerve damage


Some chemotherapy medicines, especially
oxaliplatin, damage your sensory nerves.
Called neuropathy, this damage often starts
in the hands or feet and worsens over time.
Symptoms include pain, numbness, tingling,

your risk of neuropathy, such as cold therapy.
Cold therapy involves wearing frozen
mitts (gloves) and booties (socks) during
chemotherapy infusions. The cold temperature
shrinks your blood vessels, reducing the
amount of medicine that reaches the nerves
in your hands and feet.

5 Survivorship


**Healthy habits for cancer survivors**


Eat a diet rich in plant-based foods. Limit red meat and
processed foods. A low-sugar (also called low-glycemic load
or GL) diet may help prevent the return of colon cancer. Low
GL foods cause a slower and smaller rise in blood sugar
levels than other carbohydrate-containing foods. A cancer
nutritionist can help you optimize your diet **during and after**
treatment.


Talk to your doctor about the possible risks and benefits
of long-term aspirin therapy to help prevent the return of
colorectal cancers.


Follow your primary care provider’s recommendations for
screening for other cancers based on your age, sex, and risk
level.


Get other recommended health care for your age, such
as blood pressure checks, hepatitis C screening, and
immunizations (like the flu shot).


Alcohol can increase the risk of some cancers. Drink little to
no alcohol.


Move more, rest less. Try to exercise at a moderate intensity
for at least 150 minutes per week.


Maintain a healthy weight. Tracking your weight, food intake,
and activity levels may help you meet your goals.

5 Survivorship


Key points


 - Survivorship describes the physical,
emotional, and financial issues unique to
cancer survivors. It starts at diagnosis and
continues through your lifetime.

 - A survivorship care plan is helpful in
transitioning your care to your primary
care doctor.

 - Ideally, your oncologist and primary care
provider will work together to provide
recommended follow-up care. Nurse
navigators can also be a helpful resource.

 - If you have concerns about paying for
your cancer care, financial help may be
available.

 - Healthy habits, including exercising and
eating right, play an important role in
helping to prevent other diseases and
second cancers.

Words to know


Words to know


**ablation**
A type of local therapy used to destroy tumors
in the liver or lungs. Also called image-guided
thermal ablation.

**adenocarcinoma**
The most common type of colon cancer. Starts
in cells that line the bowel and make fluids or
hormones.

**adenoma**
The most common type of colon polyp and the
most likely to form cancer cells. Also called
adenomatous polyp.

**biomarkers**
Specific features of cancer cells used to guide
treatment. Biomarkers are often mutations
(changes) in the DNA of the cancer cells.

**CAPEOX**
A chemotherapy regimen that includes
capecitabine and oxaliplatin.

**carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA)**
A protein released by some tumors that can be
detected in blood.

**chemoembolization**
Treatment for metastatic liver tumors that
delivers spheres carrying chemotherapy to the
liver vessels supplying the tumor.

**colectomy**
Surgery to remove a part of the colon.

**colon**
The first and longest section of the large
bowel. Unused food is turned into stool in the
colon.


NCCN Guidelines for Patients [®]
Colon Cancer, 2026 47



**colostomy**
Surgery to connect a part of the colon to the
outside of the abdomen and that allows stool
to drain into a bag.

**DPD deficiency**
A condition caused by an inherited difference
in the _DPYD_ gene that makes it unsafe to have
the recommended doses of fluoropyrimidine
chemotherapy medicines.

**embolization**
The use of tiny beads to cut off the blood
supply to a tumor, to deliver radiation or
chemotherapy to a tumor, or both. If radiation
and chemotherapy aren't used, it's called bland
embolization.

**familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP)**
An inherited cancer syndrome that increases
the risk of colon cancer.

**fluoropyrimidine**
A type of chemotherapy medicine. Examples
include capecitabine and fluorouracil (5-FU).

**FOLFIRI**
A chemotherapy regimen used for some
advanced colorectal cancers. Includes
leucovorin calcium, fluorouracil, and irinotecan.

**FOLFIRINOX**
A chemotherapy regimen used for some
colorectal cancers. Includes leucovorin calcium
(folinic acid), fluorouracil, irinotecan, and
oxaliplatin.

**FOLFOX**
A chemotherapy regimen that includes
leucovorin calcium, fluorouracil, and
oxaliplatin.


Words to know


**interventional oncology/radiology**
A medical specialty that uses imaging
techniques to deliver minimally invasive cancer
treatments.

**large intestine (bowel)**
A long, tube-shaped organ that forms the
last part of the digestive system. Includes the
colon, rectum, and anus.

**lymph**
A clear fluid containing white blood cells.

**lymph node**
Small groups of disease-fighting cells located
throughout the body.

**metastasis**
The spread of cancer cells from the first
(primary) tumor to a distant site.

**mismatch repair deficiency (dMMR)/high**
**microsatellite instability (MSI-H)**
A biomarker (feature) of some colon cancers
used to guide treatment. All colon cancers
should be tested for this biomarker.

**mismatch repair proficient (pMMR)/**
**microsatellite stable (MSS)**
Describes cancers that don't have the
mismatch repair deficiency (dMMR) or
the microsatellite instability-high (MSI-H)
biomarkers.

**pathologist**
A doctor who specializes in testing cells and
tissue to find disease.

_**POLE/POLD1**_ **mutation**
A biomarker (feature) of some colon cancers.
Recommended as part of comprehensive
biomarker testing for metastatic colon cancer.

**polyp**
An overgrowth of cells on the inner lining of the
colon wall. Pedunculated polyps stick out from
the bowel wall, while sessile polyps are flatter.


NCCN Guidelines for Patients [®]
Colon Cancer, 2026 48



**portal vein embolization**
A minimally invasive procedure to enlarge
the liver. May be needed if a liver resection is
planned.

**radioembolization**
A treatment for liver tumors that uses
radioactive spheres injected in the hepatic
artery (the main vessel supplying blood to the
liver). Also called selective internal radiation
therapy, or SIRT.

**rectum**
The last 5 to 6 inches of the large intestine.
Where stool is held until it exits the body
through the anus.

**recurrence**
The return of cancer after a cancer-free period.

**resectable**
Describes a cancer that can be safely removed
using surgery.

**stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT)**
A specialized type of radiation therapy used to
treat colon cancer that has spread to the liver,
lungs, or lymph nodes.

**supportive care**
A range of resources and care provided to
improve quality of life for cancer survivors.

**systemic therapy**
The use of medicines that enter the
bloodstream. Chemotherapy, targeted therapy,
and immunotherapy are types of systemic
therapy.

**unresectable**
Describes a cancer that can't be safely
removed using surgery.
